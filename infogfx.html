Nuclear Weapons

Investments by other nations over the years to modernize, expand, and advance nuclear capabilities re-enforces the reality that for our most dangerous potential adversaries, nuclear weapons play a crucial and growing role in their geostrategic ambitions. To address this reality, the U.S. nuclear deterrent capabilities must continue to confront and deter potential adversaries with the prospect of a rapid, effective, and overwhelming response that would outweigh any benefit they may hope to gain through aggression. For over 25 years, the United States has maintained and modernized the nuclear arsenal with cutting edge science and technology to effectively deter potential adversaries with a stockpile that is at the top of the pack. NNSA’s life extension programs (LEPs) underway for our aging nuclear stockpile continue to ensure that our deterrent is robust, flexible, resilient, ready, and appropriately tailored to deter 21st century threats and reassure our allies.

Counterterrorism and Counterproliferation

  • The U.S. government approach to reducing nuclear threats reflects the “prevent-counter-respond” framework to combat against current and emerging threats.
    • Prevent terrorists from developing nuclear capabilities or obtaining weapons-usable materials and technology;
    • Counter efforts by terrorists to acquire, develop, or smuggle these capabilities; and
    • Respond to nuclear incidents, whether by searching for and rendering safe a stolen foreign nuclear weapon or improvised nuclear device, or managing the consequences of a nuclear detonation, and conducting forensics and attribution activities.
  • Nuclear terrorism has evolved and the response must also evolve to meet emerging threats.
  • The U.S. will maintain the scientific, technical, and engineering capabilities to respond to nuclear terrorism and incidents involving nuclear or radiological threats.

Nonproliferation and Arms Control

The United States is, and will continue to be, a responsible nuclear weapons state. The United States is carefully monitoring any non-compliance with the Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) treaty and believes a treaty no longer benefits the collective security and stability if one Party is non-compliant with its obligations and shows no willingness to engage in meaningful discussions to address its actions. For arms control agreements to remain credible in the future, our Nation has an obligation to our citizens and our allies to hold State Parties accountable.

Our commitment to strengthening the global nonproliferation regime remains strong. With the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) at the foundation, the United States is continuing with its obligations to improve stability and prepare for further effective arms control when the security environment allows. Our U.S. nuclear forces promote strategic stability and peace by creating conditions that reduce the likelihood of escalation from crisis to conflict. NNSA has and will continue to strengthen the global nonproliferation regime and counterterrorism alliances through strategic international partnerships and collaboration. NNSA provides U.S. allies with resources and technology to promote the nation’s nonproliferation and counterterrorism agendas that include educating and training international partners to address nuclear threats and adversaries.

Nuclear Infrastruction Modernization

The United States has lost the capability and/or capacity to produce some of the strategic materials and components necessary for nuclear weapons life extensions and modernization. On an urgent basis these shortcomings must be addressed through infrastructure recapitalization programs otherwise our nation’s commitment to indefinitely maintain a resilient and effective nuclear deterrent will be threatened.

Much of the nuclear security enterprise’s infrastructure is old, obsolete, and in poor condition. More than 50% of NNSA’s facilities are more than 40 years old, nearly 30% date back to the Manhattan Project era, and 13% are currently excess and no longer needed. NNSA has invested funds into stabilizing facilities, such as new roofs, to prevent further degradation. The excess facilities constitute a drain on NNSA resources and pose risks to safety, security, and program objectives. At the end of FY2017, of the 300+ excess NNSA facilities, more than 50 were identified as high-risk.
